# Greece

## Overview

Greece, officially known as the Hellenic Republic, is a country located in Southeastern Europe, at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and Africa. It is situated on the southern tip of the Balkan Peninsula and is bordered by Albania, North Macedonia, and Bulgaria to the north, Turkey to the east, and the Aegean Sea, Ionian Sea, and Mediterranean Sea to the south and west. Greece is renowned as the cradle of Western civilization, being the birthplace of democracy, philosophy, and the Olympic Games. Its capital and largest city, Athens, is one of the world's oldest cities, with a history spanning over 3,400 years [History of Athens](https://www.britannica.com/place/Athens). As of 2023, Greece has a population of approximately 10.4 million people and covers an area of 131,957 square kilometers, including thousands of islands, of which about 227 are inhabited [Greece Facts](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/countries/greece/).

Greece is a parliamentary republic and a member of several international organizations, including the European Union (EU), NATO, and the United Nations (UN). Its economy is diverse, with key sectors including tourism, shipping, and agriculture, though it has faced significant economic challenges in recent decades [EU Member States](https://european-union.europa.eu/principles-countries-history/country-profiles/greece_en).

## Historical Background

### Ancient Greece

Greece's history is one of the longest and most influential in the world. Ancient Greece, often dated from the 12th century BCE to the 4th century BCE, laid the foundations for much of Western culture. The city-states (poleis) such as Athens, Sparta, and Corinth were central to this era. Athens is credited with developing the first known democracy around the 5th century BCE under leaders like Pericles [Ancient Greek Democracy](https://www.history.com/topics/ancient-greece/ancient-greece-democracy). This period also saw remarkable advancements in philosophy (with figures like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle), mathematics (Euclid and Archimedes), and literature (Homer’s *Iliad* and *Odyssey*).

The Hellenistic period followed the conquests of Alexander the Great (356–323 BCE), spreading Greek culture and language across much of the known world, from Egypt to India [Alexander the Great](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great). However, by 146 BCE, Greece fell under Roman control, becoming a province of the Roman Empire [Roman Greece](https://www.metmuseum.org/exhibitions/listings/2010/rome-and-the-greeks).

### Byzantine and Ottoman Periods

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire, Greece became part of the Byzantine Empire (Eastern Roman Empire) from the 4th century CE until the 15th century. During this time, Greek culture and Orthodox Christianity flourished, with Constantinople (modern-day Istanbul) as the empire’s capital. The Byzantine era preserved much of ancient Greek knowledge through monasteries and scholars [Byzantine Empire](https://www.history.com/topics/ancient-middle-east/byzantine-empire).

In 1453, the Ottoman Empire conquered Constantinople, and most of Greece fell under Ottoman rule for nearly 400 years. This period saw cultural suppression, though Greek identity persisted through language, religion, and local traditions [Ottoman Greece](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece/Ottoman-Empire-to-Modern-Greece).

### Modern Greece

The Greek War of Independence (1821–1829) marked the beginning of modern Greece, as the nation successfully fought for autonomy from the Ottoman Empire with support from European powers like Britain, France, and Russia. Greece was officially recognized as an independent state in 1830 under the Treaty of London [Greek Independence](https://www.history.com/topics/19th-century/greek-war-of-independence).

Throughout the 20th century, Greece experienced political instability, including monarchy, dictatorship, and civil war (1946–1949). It joined NATO in 1952 and the European Union in 1981, solidifying its position in the Western bloc during the Cold War [Greece in NATO](https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/topics_49212.htm). However, economic struggles, including a severe debt crisis beginning in 2009, have challenged the nation’s stability in recent decades [Greek Debt Crisis](https://www.cfr.org/backgrounder/greeces-debt-crisis-timeline).

## Current Status and Relevance

As of 2023, Greece is a developed country with a high standard of living, though it continues to recover from the economic crisis of the late 2000s and early 2010s. Its economy relies heavily on tourism—welcoming over 30 million visitors annually to sites like the Acropolis, Santorini, and Delphi—and shipping, as Greece controls one of the largest merchant fleets in the world [Tourism in Greece](https://www.unwto.org/tourism-statistics). Agriculture, particularly olive oil and feta cheese production, also plays a significant role.

Politically, Greece remains a stable democracy and an active participant in international affairs. It is a key player in regional issues, including migration (as a primary entry point for refugees into Europe) and tensions with Turkey over maritime boundaries in the Aegean Sea [Aegean Dispute](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-54679526). Greece’s strategic location also makes it a vital NATO ally, hosting U.S. military bases [Greece-NATO Relations](https://www.state.gov/u-s-relations-with-greece/).

Culturally, Greece continues to influence the world through its language, literature, and traditions. Modern Greek, a direct descendant of ancient Greek, is spoken by millions, and the country’s contributions to art, architecture, and science remain foundational to global education [Modern Greek Language](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-language).

## Notable Facts and Details

- **Geography**: Greece is composed of a mainland and over 6,000 islands and islets, with Crete being the largest. Its coastline is one of the longest in the world, stretching over 13,676 kilometers [Greek Geography](https://www.cia.gov/the-world-factbook/countries/greece/).
- **Culture**: Greek Orthodox Christianity is the predominant religion, influencing national holidays and traditions. The country is also famous for its cuisine, including dishes like moussaka, souvlaki, and baklava [Greek Culture](https://www.everyculture.com/Ge-It/Greece.html).
- **UNESCO Sites**: Greece is home to 18 UNESCO World Heritage Sites, including the Acropolis of Athens, the medieval city of Rhodes, and the archaeological site of Olympia [UNESCO Greece](https://whc.unesco.org/en/statesparties/gr).
- **Olympic Legacy**: Greece hosted the first modern Olympic Games in 1896 in Athens and again in 2004, reinforcing its historical connection to the event [Modern Olympics](https://olympics.com/en/olympic-games/athens-1896).
- **Economic Challenges**: The 2009 debt crisis led to austerity measures, high unemployment, and international bailouts, though recovery efforts have shown progress in recent years [Greek Economy](https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/GRC).
