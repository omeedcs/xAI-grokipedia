# Copenhagen

## Introduction

Copenhagen, known as *København* in Danish, is the capital and largest city of Denmark, located on the eastern coast of the island of Zealand, with parts extending onto the island of Amager. As a major cultural, economic, and political hub of Scandinavia, Copenhagen is renowned for its high quality of life, sustainable urban development, and rich historical heritage. With a population of approximately 800,000 in the city proper and over 1.3 million in the greater metropolitan area as of 2023, it serves as the heart of the Øresund Region, a cross-border metropolitan area that includes parts of southern Sweden [Statistics Denmark](https://www.dst.dk/en). Copenhagen is often celebrated for its progressive policies, vibrant arts scene, and iconic landmarks such as the Little Mermaid statue and Tivoli Gardens.

## Historical Background

Copenhagen's history dates back to the early 11th century when it was founded as a small fishing village and trading post. Its strategic location along the Øresund Strait made it a vital center for commerce during the Viking Age. The city's name, derived from the Old Norse *Køpmannæhafn*, meaning "merchants' harbor," reflects its early economic significance [Visit Copenhagen](https://www.visitcopenhagen.com).

- **Medieval Growth**: By the 12th century, Copenhagen had grown into a fortified town under the control of the Catholic Church. Bishop Absalon, often credited as the city's founder, constructed a castle and fortifications in 1167 to protect the growing settlement from pirate attacks [Danish History](https://denmark.dk/people-and-culture/history).
- **Capital Status**: In 1443, Copenhagen officially became the capital of Denmark, replacing Roskilde. During the reign of Christian IV in the early 17th century, the city underwent significant expansion, with the construction of landmarks like Rosenborg Castle and the Børsen stock exchange [Rosenborg Castle](https://www.kongernesamling.dk/en/rosenborg/).
- **Industrial Era and Modernization**: The 19th and 20th centuries saw Copenhagen transform into an industrial and cultural powerhouse. The city endured challenges such as the Napoleonic Wars and World War II, including the German occupation from 1940 to 1945, but emerged as a leader in design, architecture, and urban planning post-war [National Museum of Denmark](https://en.natmus.dk/historical-knowledge/denmark/).

## Current Status and Relevance

Today, Copenhagen is a global leader in sustainability, innovation, and livability. Frequently ranked among the world's most livable cities by indices such as the *Economist Intelligence Unit*, it is known for its extensive cycling infrastructure, green spaces, and commitment to carbon neutrality by 2025 [City of Copenhagen](https://international.kk.dk). The city serves as the political center of Denmark, housing the Danish Parliament at Christiansborg Palace, and is a key player in international diplomacy and trade within the European Union.

- **Economy**: Copenhagen's economy is diverse, with strengths in shipping (home to Maersk, the world's largest container shipping company), pharmaceuticals, technology, and renewable energy. The city also hosts numerous international conferences and events, reinforcing its status as a hub for innovation [Copenhagen Business Hub](https://www.copcap.com).
- **Culture**: The city is a cultural epicenter, boasting world-class museums like the National Museum of Denmark, cutting-edge culinary scenes with restaurants like Noma, and a vibrant music and arts community. Copenhagen's design heritage, exemplified by brands like LEGO and Bang & Olufsen, is globally recognized [Wonderful Copenhagen](https://www.wonderfulcopenhagen.com).
- **Sustainability**: Copenhagen's ambitious environmental policies include extensive public transport systems, wind energy initiatives, and urban planning focused on reducing carbon emissions. Over 60% of its residents commute by bicycle, supported by more than 400 kilometers of dedicated bike lanes [Cycling Embassy of Denmark](http://www.cycling-embassy.dk).

## Notable Facts and Details

- **Landmarks**: Copenhagen is home to iconic sites such as the Little Mermaid statue, inspired by Hans Christian Andersen's fairy tale, and Nyhavn, a picturesque 17th-century waterfront lined with colorful buildings. Tivoli Gardens, opened in 1843, is one of the world's oldest amusement parks and a major tourist attraction [Tivoli Gardens](https://www.tivoli.dk/en).
- **Education and Research**: The University of Copenhagen, founded in 1479, is one of the oldest and most prestigious universities in Northern Europe, contributing to the city's reputation as a center for academic excellence [University of Copenhagen](https://www.ku.dk/english/).
- **Population Diversity**: While historically homogenous, Copenhagen has become increasingly diverse, with significant immigrant communities contributing to its cultural fabric. As of recent data, around 25% of the population has a non-Danish background [Statistics Denmark](https://www.dst.dk/en/Statistik/emner/borgere/befolkning).
- **Climate**: The city experiences a temperate oceanic climate, with mild summers (average highs of 20°C/68°F) and cool winters (average lows of 0°C/32°F). Its coastal location makes it prone to windy conditions [Danish Meteorological Institute](https://www.dmi.dk/en/).

## Related Topics

- **Øresund Bridge**: A combined railway and motorway bridge connecting Copenhagen to Malmö, Sweden, symbolizing the close ties between Denmark and Sweden within the Øresund Region [Øresund Bridge](https://www.oresundsbron.com/en).
- **Hans Christian Andersen**: The famous Danish author, whose works like *The Little Mermaid* and *The Ugly Duckling* are deeply tied to Copenhagen's cultural identity [Hans Christian Andersen Museum](https://hcandersen-homepage.dk).
- **Nordic Model**: Copenhagen exemplifies the Nordic model of governance, characterized by a strong welfare state, high taxes, and social equality, influencing its policies and societal structure [Nordic Council](https://www.norden.org/en).
- **Scandinavian Design**: The city's contributions to minimalist and functional design are evident in its architecture, furniture, and public spaces, aligning with broader Scandinavian design principles [Design Museum Denmark](https://designmuseum.dk/en/).
